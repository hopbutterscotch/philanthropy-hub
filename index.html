Orientation

Read this first. This is the philosophy of the role before you start planning events.

The Bottom Line

Philanthropy is about reaching out, making the chapter visible, and generating revenue. Planning matters, but planning can quickly become overly complicated. Your job is to simplify, push back against unnecessary complexity, and stay focused on what actually puts money in your hands.

If a task is too many steps away from real cash, do something else. With a tight budget, make everything visible, gimmicky, colorful, and engaging. A simple event can work if you dress it up.

1

Choose Simple Events

Do not reinvent the wheel. Look for the lowest-hanging fruit: an event that is already financially realistic, logistically available, and easy for people to understand.

Examples:
  • A raffle works better when it has a countdown, prize reveal, or public drawing.
  • A basic table works better when it has a game, wheel, challenge, or funny sign.
  • A tournament works better with brackets, team names, rivalry, and visible updates.
2

Dress Up the Event

The event itself is not always what determines revenue. What matters is how you package it, sell it, and make others see it. Add bells and whistles. Add color. Make the event look active.

Examples:
  • Use a visible donation goal board.
  • Create competition between groups.
  • Make the prize or outcome public.
  • Make people feel like they are joining something, not just donating.
3

Reach Out Relentlessly

Know your audience and adjust your message, but never hesitate. Anyone, anywhere can be a prospect. Ask, call, text, email, and follow up.

Think in numbers:
  • If 10% of asks become yeses, then 100 asks can become 10 yeses.
  • If 1,000 asks are made, that can become 100 yeses.
  • Your job is to collect the "no's" until enough yeses come in.
4

Make Participation Easy, Then Demand It

Think accessibility, incentive, deterrence, and ownership. Volunteering should practically fall into the brothers' laps, but do not be afraid to demand participation.

Ask yourself:
  • Is helping easy and clear?
  • What incentivizes brothers to help?
  • What discourages them from skipping?
  • Do they feel ownership over the event?
5

Promote With Repetition

Once the event is chosen and dressed up, the next step is unwavering repetition. Ask, call, post, text, email. Then repeat that loop.

Visibility partners:
  • Sororities
  • Other UNM clubs
  • Professors
  • Alumni
  • Local businesses
  • Friends and family networks
6

Minimize Cost to Maximize Profit

Events are about image, energy, and show. Materials do not need to last forever. They usually only need to survive the event. Avoid buying high-quality materials when cheap materials will do the job.

Before spending, ask:
  • Do we already own this?
  • Can a brother bring it?
  • Can we borrow it?
  • Can a business donate it?
  • Can AI or research help us make this cheaper?
7

Lead on Event Day

Event day is where you realize leadership matters more than the plan. The plan is useful, but it is not a safety net. Something will almost always go differently than expected.

You are the person most fit to lead because you know the most about the event. Take control, improvise when necessary, and protect the real goal: generating revenue.

Examples:
  • If foot traffic is weak, move or adjust the pitch.
  • If brothers do not show up, simplify the setup.
  • If supplies are missing, substitute quickly.
  • If the plan stops helping revenue, change the plan.
8

Record Results Immediately

Record your results while they are fresh and in front of you. Do not wait. Receipts disappear, screenshots get buried, brothers forget details, and materials get thrown away.

Record before you move on:
  • Gross revenue
  • Costs
  • Net profit
  • Receipts
  • What worked
  • What should change next time
9

Be Professional With Partners

When working with businesses, alumni, campus organizations, or beneficiaries, remember that you represent the event, the chapter, and the cause. Look presentable. Be confident. Be professional.

Keep the ask simple:
  • Explain what the event is.
  • Explain who it benefits.
  • Explain what you are asking for.
  • Follow up and send a thank-you after the event.
10

Leave the Role Better Than You Found It

At the end of your term, think about what you wish you had known before you started. That knowledge is valuable. Pass it on.

Your handoff is how you leave a permanent influence on the position. The next chairman should not have to start from scratch.

Playbooks

Simple planning outlines. Edit them as the chapter learns what works.

Raffle

  • Confirm prize and rules.
  • Create a sales tracker.
  • Promote with a clear deadline.
  • Record winner, revenue, costs, and lessons learned.

Tournament

  • Reserve location.
  • Create bracket and rules.
  • Assign check-in, referee, and cleanup roles.
  • Record teams, revenue, costs, and issues.

Business Outreach

  • Build a target list.
  • Use one clear sponsorship ask.
  • Track follow-ups.
  • Send a thank-you and impact summary.

Golf Ball Drop

  • Confirm location and safety requirements.
  • Track ticket and ball numbers carefully.
  • Assign judges and recorder.
  • Save final results and process notes.
